Plate Tectonics – Leaving Cert Geography

Key Terms

  • Lithosphere: rigid shell of crust and upper mantle.
  • Asthenosphere: semi-molten, ductile zone that allows plates to move.
  • Mesosphere: rigid lower mantle.
  • Moho: crust–mantle boundary.
  • Gutenberg Discontinuity: mantle–outer core boundary.
  • Lehmann Discontinuity: outer–inner core boundary.
  • Subduction: oceanic plate sinking beneath a continental plate.
  • Seafloor Spreading: creation of new oceanic crust at mid-ocean ridges.
  • Paleomagnetism: record of Earth’s magnetic reversals in rocks.
  • Slab Pull: sinking plate drags the rest of the plate behind it.
  • Ridge Push: gravitational sliding away from mid-ocean ridges.
  • Benioff Zone: inclined earthquake zone at subduction boundaries.
  • Hotspot: plume of magma from mantle forming volcanic islands.

The Layers of the Earth (Detailed)

1. Crust

  • Continental Crust: 30–70 km, granite, light, less dense, up to 4 billion years old.
  • Oceanic Crust: 5–10 km, basalt, denser, younger (<200 million years).

2. Mantle

Depth ~2,900 km, made of silicate rocks rich in iron and magnesium.

  • Upper Mantle: rigid, part of lithosphere.
  • Asthenosphere: semi-molten, 100–350 km depth, convection currents occur here.
  • Mesosphere: lower mantle, solid but slowly convecting.

3. Core

  • Outer Core: 2,900–5,150 km, liquid iron & nickel, generates Earth’s magnetic field.
  • Inner Core: 5,150–6,371 km, solid due to pressure, temp ~5,500°C.

Plate Movements

Divergent (Constructive Boundaries)

Plates move apart as convection currents rise in the mantle. Magma emerges, forming new crust.

  • Mid-Atlantic Ridge: Separates Eurasian & North American Plates, and African & South American Plates.
  • Iceland: volcanic island on the ridge; geothermal energy and frequent eruptions.

Convergent (Destructive Boundaries)

Plates collide. Oceanic crust subducts beneath continental crust, or continents collide to form mountains.

  • Andes Mountains: Nazca Plate subducts beneath South America.
  • Himalayas: Indian Plate collides with Eurasian Plate, forming the highest fold mountains.
  • The Alps: Africa pushing into Eurasia.
  • Wicklow Mountains: ancient Caledonian fold mountains in Ireland.

Transform (Conservative Boundaries)

Plates slide past each other without creating or destroying crust.

  • San Andreas Fault: Pacific Plate sliding past North American Plate; major earthquakes occur here.

Landforms from Plate Movements

  • Mid-Ocean Ridges: e.g., Mid-Atlantic Ridge, Iceland.
  • Volcanic Island Arcs: e.g., Japan, Philippines, Caribbean.
  • Ocean Trenches: e.g., Peru–Chile Trench off South America.
  • Fold Mountains: Andes, Himalayas, Alps, and Caledonian mountains in Ireland.

Study Tips

  • Use labelled diagrams of Earth’s layers and plate boundaries.
  • Always name the plates in your answer (e.g., Nazca Plate, Eurasian Plate).
  • Link the process to landforms (subduction → trenches + fold mountains).
  • Include case studies like Iceland, Andes, Himalayas, San Andreas Fault.
  • Practice definitions of key terms like subduction, slab pull, hotspot.
